DE BEERS IN BOTSWANA

Before the discovery of one the world’s most valued and sought after mineral
in Botswana it is safe to say the country was in dire straits. Most of the
country often rarely sees enough rain to this day which leads to draught being
a common theme in the agricultural sphere. Botswana had a very small 
pollution at the time meaning human capital was also an issue. In 1966 when
Botswana got its independence from the British it was estimated there were
only about 40 university graduates and about 100 secondary school graduates
in a population of 595,000. Along with the often erratic rainfall the country
was highly undeveloped and officially one of the poorest nations in the world
while showing little economic potential.
Then something would happen that changed the countries fortunes
significantly, the discovery of diamond deposits by De Beers the dominant
seller rough stones in the world. According to an article on the De Beers
website (http://www.debeersgroup.com) the company secured prospecting
rights in Botswana dating back to 1938 and by 1955 exploration was ongoing
consistently. After many years of coming up empty the company began
considering its withdrawal from Botswana. A year before Botswana would gain
its independence one of their geologist by the name of Dr Gavin Lamont
managed to persuade his superiors to continue searching for at least one more
year. If no diamond were to be found by the winter of 1966 then all
exploration in Botswana would come to an end.

With only two months to go before the deadline the first kimberlite pipe was found in the Mochudi area. For those unfamiliar with what kimberlite pipes are here is a brief description {Gems can be formed within the Earth’s mantle and it is only here that gem quality diamonds can be created. Diamonds that originate in the mantle are created when heat and pressure transform carbon.
The mantle is nearly 100 miles below the Earth’s surface, and gems that come from there are brought to the surface by kimberlite pipes, which are formed by
deep-source volcanic eruptions.

Diamond Exploration companies tend to focus their efforts on finding
kimberlite pipes to help uncover diamond deposits. So one year after finding
the first kimberlite pipe around the Mochudi area diamonds were discovered
in Orapa. It is from this point on things began to take a turn for the better in
Botswana and De Beers relationship with the country would begin to blossom.
De Beers would go on to discover the 3 major mines in Botswana located in
Orapa, Letlhakane and Jwaneng respectively, of which Jwaneng is now the
world’s richest diamond mine by value. It is worth noting that there are fifteen diamond mining countries in Africa.
They are: Botswana, South Africa, Angola, Namibia, Congo, Zimbabwe, Guinea,
Ghana, Congo, Lesotho, Liberia, Sierra Leone, CAR, Tanzania, and Togo.
Of  these fifteen Botswana is the top producer of diamonds in Africa, until 2014 
Botswana was the top producer in the world before Russia overtook them.
Diamonds make up about 75% of the country’s foreign exchange earnings and
30% of its GDP. The governments Botswana along with Namibia both have
50/50 joint venture partnerships with De beers to mine diamonds their
respective countries. This is a far cry from many diamond mining countries
such as war torn Congo and different faction’s wheel and deal with little
benefit to the country and its people. Some African countries get profit as little
as 8 % from minerals produced from their lands due to issues like corruption
poor dealings or a general lack of development.
Thanks to a politically stable environment and through shrewd development
and management of its diamond resources in conjunction with long-term
development in mind Botswana and its diamond industry has witnessed much
growth and prosperity. Diamond resources play a key role in infrastructure and
human capital development. Since independence the resources have been
invested in important national issues such as water and transport
infrastructure, education, healthcare and public spending on social services is
still heavily funded by diamond revenues.
